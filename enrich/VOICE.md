# The Gottdank story voice

Every person's `story` in `family.js` follows these rules. They are not optional.

## Voice
- **Spoken register.** Write like you're telling a cousin at lunch. Contractions, plain verbs, varied sentence length. At most one carved/striking line per person, and only at a turn.
- **No em dashes. Ever.** Use commas, periods, or the word "and". (The long dash character is banned.)
- **No double-quotes inside the prose.** Use single quotes for any quoted words (the file's strings are double-quoted).
- **Open on a concrete fact or action.** Never a summary, a virtue ("a loving father"), or a meta line about the tree, the register, or research.
- **No process narration.** Never mention research, sources, "records show", "fully documented", or how anything was found.
- **Facts, not adjectives.** Show the thing; don't label it.
- **Warm uncertainty.** "the story in the family is", or "likely" used once. Never "we know", never stacked hedging.
- **Cite or skip.** Every concrete claim must come from the existing entry or your research. If a fact isn't supported, leave it out. Never fabricate to fill space.

## What the ideal story does
A reader who's never heard of this person should finish feeling like they stood in that person's time and place. The facts are the skeleton; the era is the flesh. Five beats:
1. **Open in-scene** on a concrete, characteristic fact or image.
2. **Set the world** around them, the town, the decade, the trade, the historical moment, all sourced, so the reader is transported.
3. **Weave their real life through it**, the dated arc (born, worked, married, moved, the kids, the war), braided into that world so their choices connect to the era.
4. **One carved line** at the turn of their life. Just one.
5. **Land it honestly**, where the line goes, the loss in the flattest sentence, or the continuation. End on life.

You may be expansive about two things only: the sourced WORLD they lived in, and their real DOCUMENTED facts. Never invent the individual's feelings, thoughts, or undocumented events. That is what lets a thin person still get a real story: the world around them is documented even when they aren't.

## Make it sing (craft)
Clean and true is the floor. Good writing is the bar. Every story should earn its place:
- **Earn one line.** Each story has exactly ONE sentence worth remembering, and it lands at the turn. Not ornate, TRUE, the kind that reframes everything (Kalman becomes Coleman becomes Cole; trading the exurbs for the marsh and the live oaks). If no line stands out, you haven't found the story yet.
- **Specifics are the craft.** A named street, a year, a trade, a boat, a price beats every adjective. Concrete detail does the work; description doesn't.
- **Rhythm.** Vary sentence length. Wind out a long one, then cut hard with a short one. Read it aloud in your head; if it's flat, reshape it.
- **Cut every limp sentence.** If a sentence only carries a dry fact and adds no image or motion, fold it into a stronger one or cut it. No connective filler.
- **End on resonance.** The last line lands, an image, a turn, or the line of descent continuing. Never trail off on a minor fact.
- **No two stories the same shape.** Let each person's real facts dictate the structure. If it reads like a template, rewrite it.

## No résumé. No corporate boilerplate.
This is the big one. **Never describe what someone's employer does, its acquisitions, headcount, revenue, products, or business.** Nobody cares that a company moved 4,200 people across 47 countries, or what the software logs, or how many organizations it served. That is the worst kind of filler: it fills space while telling you nothing about the person.

Name what the PERSON does in one plain, human line, then move on to *them*: where they live, who they love, how they fit the family, what they do with their own time. A person is not their LinkedIn. If the only public record is a job, write a short human paragraph anchored in place and family, not a long one padded with career facts.

## Living people
- Public record only, nothing private.
- **Lead with who they are and how they fit the family and the place they've made their life, not their job.** Work is one light line at most.
- Keep it tight, warm, and human. A living person with a thin contemporary record gets a SHORT true paragraph, not a long padded one. Better short and human than long and corporate.

## Hard history
Holocaust, dead children: establish the ordinary life first, name the loss in the flattest sentence, don't linger, end on life. Lightest possible touch for children.

## Length
- **Documented people in a vivid era** (mostly the deceased, the immigrant generations): a short biographical essay, 3 to 5 short paragraphs, immersive per the five beats. Length comes from real history and real facts woven together, never from padding.
- **Thin or living people:** tighter, 1 to 2 warm human paragraphs. Never pad to hit a length, and never with corporate facts.
